❓ Question 1: Where exactly is it located in the Louvre?

The Raft of the Medusa is displayed in the Denon Wing of the Louvre, on Level 1, in Room 700 (Salle Mollien), in the French Romantic painting section.

The canvas is enormous: about 4.91 m high and 7.16 m wide. When visitors enter the room, the painting dominates the space like a giant screen, which makes the tragedy even more impressive.

Location
❓ Question 2: What real event inspired this painting?

The painting is based on the shipwreck of the French frigate Méduse in 1816, near the coast of present-day Mauritania, on the way to Senegal.

Because of poor navigation and bad decisions, the ship ran aground. A large group of people were placed on a makeshift wooden raft. About 147 people started on the raft; after 13 days at sea, only 15 survived, after hunger, thirst, madness and even cannibalism. The scandal shocked France and became a symbol of political incompetence.

❓ Question 3: How did Géricault prepare this painting?

Géricault worked almost like an investigator or an engineer:

  • He interviewed survivors such as Savigny (the doctor) and Corréard (the engineer).
  • He read official reports and newspapers about the disaster.
  • He built a scale model of the raft to study its structure.
  • He observed dead bodies in hospitals to paint the anatomy realistically.

He combined all this information to design a composition that is both historically based and visually powerful.

Research & method
❓ Question 4: What moment of the story is shown in the painting?

The scene shows the moment when the survivors finally see a rescue ship on the horizon and try to signal it. Some men wave pieces of cloth, others stand up and stretch their arms toward the tiny ship in the distance.

The composition is built like a double pyramid: dead bodies in the foreground form the base, while the group of men climbing toward the top expresses hope. At the same time, the dark sea and sky keep the feeling of danger and instability.

❓ Question 5: How can we see this story as a system failure?

From a systems engineering point of view, the Méduse is a complete system breakdown:

  • Poor leadership: an inexperienced captain was chosen for political reasons, not for competence.
  • Bad risk management: navigation errors and no proper contingency plan for a shipwreck.
  • Inadequate resources: the raft was badly designed, overloaded and had almost no food or water.
  • Ethical collapse: the crew abandoned people on the raft to save themselves.

The catastrophe is not just “bad luck”; it is the consequence of a series of design and decision failures in the whole socio-technical system.

System failure
❓ Question 6: What subsystems and mission can we identify?

We can describe the situation using typical systems engineering concepts:

  • Mission: transport people safely from France to Senegal.
  • Human subsystem: officers, sailors, soldiers, passengers, plus their skills, decisions and behaviors.
  • Technical subsystem: the ship, the emergency boats, and finally the raft (structure, ropes, mast, sails).
  • Environmental subsystem: ocean, weather, currents, visibility.
  • Management & governance subsystem: the Navy, political decisions, rules for safety and evacuation.

The painting shows what happens when these subsystems are not correctly designed, coordinated or controlled. It is a visual case study of a failed complex system.
