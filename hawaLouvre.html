❓ Question 1: Which painting are we talking about?

We are talking about the Mona Lisa, also known as La Joconde. It was painted by Leonardo da Vinci in the early 16th century. The painting shows a woman with a calm expression and a mysterious, almost enigmatic smile.

The model is believed to be Lisa del Giocondo, a Florentine woman. Today the Mona Lisa is displayed in the Louvre Museum in Paris and is one of the most famous works of art in the world.

❓ Question 2: What is the interesting story behind it?

In 1911, the Mona Lisa was stolen from the Louvre. A worker named Vincenzo Peruggia hid inside the museum, removed the painting from its frame, and walked out with it under his coat.

For two years, nobody knew where the painting was. In 1913, Peruggia tried to sell it in Italy and was arrested. When the Mona Lisa returned to Paris, the scandal made it even more famous worldwide.

❓ Question 3: How can we describe it using systems engineering?

In systems engineering, the Mona Lisa is not only a painting—it's the heart of a complex system:

  • The mission is: “Allow visitors to see the Mona Lisa while keeping it safe for centuries.”
  • The stakeholders include: visitors, museum staff, conservators, security teams, the French state, and future generations.
  • The technical architecture contains: a climate-controlled glass case, cameras, sensors, alarms, lighting, barriers, and visitor-flow control.
  • The human subsystem includes: security guards, engineers, curators, and people who design the visitor space.

All components must work together to satisfy requirements like security, conservation, comfort, and accessibility. This is a perfect example of how systems engineers think: define the mission, identify stakeholders, extract requirements, and design a coherent architecture.
